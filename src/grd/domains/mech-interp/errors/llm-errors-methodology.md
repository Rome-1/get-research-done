# LLM Mechanistic Interpretability Error Catalog — Methodology Classes (#26-50)

Errors in experimental design, statistical analysis, and interpretation methodology specific to mechanistic interpretability research.

## Error Classes

| # | Error Class | Description | Detection Strategy | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 26 | **Selection bias in dataset construction** | LLMs construct evaluation datasets that are biased toward easy or typical cases, making circuits appear more complete and features more interpretable than they are on the full distribution. | Verify dataset diversity: does it include edge cases, ambiguous inputs, and adversarial examples? Compare circuit faithfulness on the curated dataset vs a random sample from the model's training distribution. Report both numbers. | LLM tests the IOI circuit on 100 examples all of the form "When [name1] and [name2] went to [place], [name2] gave..." — same template every time. The circuit works perfectly. But on varied templates ("After [name1] left [name2] at the...", "[name1], who was with [name2], then..."), faithfulness drops to 60%. |
| 27 | **P-hacking through metric selection** | LLMs try multiple metrics (logit difference, probability, KL divergence, loss) and report whichever gives the most impressive result, without adjusting for multiple comparisons. Different metrics can give contradictory importance rankings. | Pre-register the metric before running experiments. If multiple metrics are reported, apply Bonferroni correction or report all of them transparently. Verify that the main conclusion holds under at least two different metrics. | LLM reports "head 9.1 accounts for 45% of the effect" using probability difference. Under logit difference, the same head accounts for 22%. Under KL divergence, 31%. Only the 45% number is reported because it's most impressive. |
| 28 | **Confusing direct and indirect effects** | LLMs report a component's total effect (how much the output changes when it's ablated) as its direct effect (its direct contribution to the output logit). The total effect includes indirect effects through all downstream components that read the ablated component's output. | Compute both: (1) direct effect = component_output · W_U[token] (the component's direct contribution to the logit). (2) total effect = change in logit when component is ablated (includes downstream effects). If total >> direct, the component's influence is mainly through downstream components. | LLM ablates head 7.3 and the logit difference drops by 4.0, so reports "head 7.3 contributes 4.0 to the logit." But head 7.3's direct logit attribution is only 0.3 — most of its effect is indirect, through influencing heads in layers 8-11 via the residual stream. |
| 29 | **Not controlling for tokenization** | LLMs assume consistent tokenization across examples in a dataset. But different prompts may tokenize the same word differently (e.g., " Paris" vs "Paris" vs "paris"), placing the relevant information at different sub-token positions and confounding position-based analysis. | Check tokenization for every example in the dataset. Verify that the target token is consistently tokenized. If it splits into multiple sub-tokens in some examples, either filter those examples or account for the multi-token case explicitly. | LLM creates an IOI dataset where "Mary" tokenizes as one token in most examples but as ["Mar", "y"] in 15% of examples (due to different preceding tokens). The circuit analysis assumes the name is at one position, but in 15% of cases the relevant information is split across two positions, reducing measured faithfulness. |
| 30 | **Overclaiming interpretability of dead features** | LLMs analyze features that rarely activate (density <0.001%) and assign confident interpretations based on the few examples where they fire. With so few activations, any apparent pattern may be coincidental. | Flag features with activation density below 0.01% as unreliable for interpretation. For rare features, require that the interpretation correctly predicts activation on synthetically generated examples (not just explains the observed activations). Report activation density alongside every interpretation. | LLM finds feature #12847 activates on exactly 3 examples in a 1M-token corpus: "quantum entanglement", "quantum computing", "quantum mechanics." Labels it a "quantum physics feature" with high confidence. But with only 3 examples, this could be coincidence — the feature might also activate on unseen text about "quantum of solace" or "quantum leap" (the TV show). |
| 31 | **Ignoring training data contamination** | LLMs interpret features and circuits without considering that the model may have memorized specific training examples. A "factual recall circuit" may be a memorization circuit; a "reasoning feature" may detect memorized reasoning patterns. | Test whether the circuit/feature generalizes to novel inputs that are structurally similar but factually different from likely training data. Compare performance on common facts (likely in training data) vs rare facts (likely not). If a "reasoning circuit" only works on problems with known solutions, it may be doing retrieval, not reasoning. | LLM discovers a "mathematical reasoning circuit" that achieves 95% accuracy on common textbook problems. But on novel problems with the same structure and difficulty, accuracy drops to 40%. The circuit is primarily doing pattern-matching to memorized solutions, not general reasoning. |
| 32 | **Averaging over heterogeneous mechanisms** | LLMs average patching results across examples that use different internal mechanisms, washing out the signal from each. The model may use different circuits for different sub-types of the same task. | Cluster examples by their patching profiles (which components are important) before averaging. If there are distinct clusters, report results per cluster. A bimodal distribution of patching effects suggests multiple mechanisms. | LLM averages activation patching results for "factual recall" across both common facts (recalled from MLPs) and rare facts (inferred from context). The averaged trace shows weak, diffuse localization because two distinct mechanisms are being averaged together. Separately, each mechanism shows clean localization. |
| 33 | **Wrong temperature/sampling interaction** | LLMs analyze model behavior at temperature=0 (greedy decoding) but draw conclusions about the model's behavior in general. At non-zero temperature, the probability distribution over tokens matters, not just the argmax. Circuits that determine the top-1 token may differ from those that shape the full distribution. | Specify the temperature/sampling settings for all analyses. If conclusions are about the model's general behavior, verify they hold at the temperature used in deployment (often T=0.7-1.0). For probability-based metrics, temperature cancels in logit differences but affects probability differences. | LLM identifies a circuit for "story completion" at T=0 that always produces the most likely continuation. At T=1.0, the model uses a richer distribution where multiple continuations have similar probability, and the circuit only captures the mode, missing the distributional richness that defines creative generation. |
| 34 | **Conflating model editing with understanding** | LLMs claim that successful model editing (e.g., ROME, MEMIT changing a fact) demonstrates understanding of how the model stores that fact. But editing can succeed by brute-force overwriting without the edit being mechanistically faithful to the model's natural storage mechanism. | Verify that the editing method is faithful: (1) does the edit generalize to paraphrases of the fact? (2) does it preserve unrelated facts? (3) does the edited model's internal mechanism match the unedited model's (just with different content)? If the edit breaks other facts or only works for exact wording, it's brute-force overwriting. | LLM edits "The president of X is Y" → "The president of X is Z" using rank-one model editing, and reports "this demonstrates that the model stores presidential facts in the MLP at layer 17." But the edit also changes responses to "The capital of X is..." and "X's most famous landmark is..." — it corrupted the entire entity representation, not just the presidential fact. |
| 35 | **Ignoring BOS token effects** | LLMs forget that many models use a beginning-of-sequence (BOS) token that accumulates garbage attention and serves as a "null position" for attention heads with nothing relevant to attend to. Features and attention patterns at the BOS position are often artifacts, not meaningful computation. | When analyzing attention patterns, note which heads attend to the BOS position and why. Don't interpret BOS-focused attention as "the head attends to the beginning of the sentence." When probing or patching, handle the BOS position separately from content positions. | LLM finds that head 3.7 attends almost entirely to position 0 and labels it a "sentence-beginning feature detector." But position 0 is the BOS token, and the head is just using it as a no-op (dumping attention there when there's nothing useful to attend to). The head's OV circuit produces near-zero output regardless of what's at position 0. |
| 36 | **Not distinguishing pre-trained vs fine-tuned behavior** | LLMs analyze a fine-tuned model (RLHF, instruction-tuned) and attribute findings to the base pre-trained model. Fine-tuning can rearrange circuits, add new pathways, and change feature interpretations compared to the base model. | Specify whether the model is base or fine-tuned. If analyzing a fine-tuned model, verify findings on the base model too. Features and circuits that exist only after fine-tuning are fine-tuning artifacts, not properties of the pre-training distribution. | LLM discovers a "refusal circuit" in an RLHF model and claims it's "how transformers handle safety." But this circuit was created by RLHF fine-tuning — the base model has no such circuit. The finding is about the fine-tuning process, not about transformers in general. |
| 37 | **Wrong virtual weight computation** | LLMs incorrectly compose weight matrices when computing "virtual weights" (the effective linear map through multiple transformer components). Common errors: wrong matrix multiplication order, forgetting to include layer norm, transposing the wrong matrix. | Verify virtual weight computation by comparing W_virtual @ x against the actual model output for several test inputs x. The numerical values should match to float precision. Check matrix dimensions at each step: W_Q is (d_head, d_model), W_K is (d_head, d_model), W_V is (d_head, d_model), W_O is (d_model, d_head). Matrix multiplication order depends on whether you're computing QK or OV circuits. | LLM computes the OV circuit as W_O @ W_V but should compute it as W_O @ W_V (with correct shapes). In TransformerLens: W_OV = W_V @ W_O where W_V is (d_head, d_model) and W_O is (d_model, d_head). Getting the transpose wrong gives a matrix of the wrong shape that still "works" numerically if both dimensions happen to be equal (as in GPT-2 where d_head can equal d_model/n_heads). |
| 38 | **Confirmation bias in feature labeling** | LLMs interpret SAE features by looking at max-activating examples and finding a pattern that confirms their prior expectation, ignoring examples that contradict the interpretation. This is especially common when the analyst has a hypothesis about what the feature "should" represent. | Use blind labeling: present max-activating examples to an annotator who doesn't know the hypothesized label. Check inter-annotator agreement. Use automated interpretability scoring that doesn't depend on a pre-existing hypothesis. Always report the fraction of activating examples that DON'T match the proposed label. | LLM is looking for "Python syntax" features and finds feature #8421 activates on several Python code examples. Labels it "Python function definitions." But 40% of activating examples are actually JavaScript, Java, or pseudocode — the feature responds to programming language function syntax generally, not Python specifically. |
| 39 | **Not accounting for floating point precision** | LLMs treat neural network computations as exact, but float32 and float16 arithmetic introduces numerical errors that matter for fine-grained analysis. Attribution values can differ between forward passes due to non-deterministic GPU operations, and very small attribution values may be numerical noise rather than real signal. | Report numerical precision of results. For attribution values, verify stability by running the same computation 3 times and checking variance. Attributions smaller than 1e-5 (float32) or 1e-3 (float16) may be noise. When summing many small attributions, verify the total against an independent computation. | LLM reports that 47 features each contribute 0.001-0.01 to the logit difference and concludes all 47 are "relevant." But the numerical noise floor at float32 is ~1e-5, and running the same analysis three times gives different sets of "relevant" features below magnitude 0.05. Only features with attributions consistently above the noise floor are meaningfully contributing. |
| 40 | **Wrong attention pattern interpretation at multi-token words** | LLMs interpret attention to "position 5" as attending to the word at position 5, but tokenization may split words across multiple tokens. The model may attend to the second sub-token of a multi-token word, and the relevant position is the first sub-token (where the word-level embedding is most influenced by the actual word). | For every attention pattern analysis, map token positions back to the original words. When a word is split into multiple tokens, note which sub-token receives the attention. The first sub-token usually carries the most semantic information; attention to later sub-tokens may indicate the model is reading the full word versus just using the first sub-token's embedding. | LLM visualizes attention and finds head 7.2 "attends to the blank position between words." But the "blank" is actually the space token that precedes the next word — in GPT-2's tokenization, many words begin with a space character. The head is attending to the start of a word, not to whitespace. |
| 41 | **Overcounting feature overlap** | LLMs count unique features across layers/positions without checking whether the same feature (or highly correlated features) appear at multiple sites. This inflates the apparent circuit size and complexity. | Compute cosine similarity between feature decoder directions across layers. Features with cosine similarity >0.8 likely represent the same concept at different layers. Report both the raw feature count and the deduplicated count. | LLM reports a circuit with "47 distinct features across 6 layers." But 15 of those features have decoder directions with >0.85 cosine similarity to features in adjacent layers — they're the same concept propagated through the residual stream. The effective circuit has ~32 distinct features. |
| 42 | **Ignoring output normalization** | LLMs forget that models typically apply a final layer norm before the unembedding. Logit attribution that ignores this normalization is inaccurate — each component's contribution is scaled by the layer norm's gain, which depends on the total residual stream norm. | Include the final layer norm in all logit computations. Direct logit attribution without accounting for layer norm gives approximately correct rankings but incorrect magnitudes. For precise attribution, linearize the layer norm around the operating point: contribution_adjusted = contribution × (d_norm/d_resid) evaluated at the current residual stream value. | LLM computes direct logit attribution as head_output · W_U and finds attribution values that sum to 12.5, but the actual logit difference is 8.3. The discrepancy is from the final layer norm, which scales each component's contribution non-uniformly based on the residual stream norm. |
| 43 | **Single-step causal tracing conclusions** | LLMs run causal tracing that restores activations at one (layer, position) site at a time and conclude these sites are the complete set of important locations. But restoring one site can have nonlinear interactions — restoring two sites together may have superadditive or subadditive effects that single-site tracing misses. | After identifying important sites via single-site tracing, test multi-site restoration: (1) restore the top-2 sites together — is the effect greater than the sum? (2) restore all identified sites — does this achieve near-complete recovery? If single-site effects sum to more than 100%, there's redundancy. If they sum to much less than 100%, there are interaction effects. | LLM finds 5 sites with individual restoration effects of 40%, 35%, 25%, 20%, 15% = 135% total. Concludes "any 3 sites suffice." But restoring the top-3 together achieves only 60% recovery (subadditive) because sites 1 and 2 carry partially redundant information. Full recovery requires all 5 sites. |
| 44 | **Not reporting compute costs** | LLMs describe expensive analyses without noting computational requirements, making results unreproducible for researchers with fewer resources. SAE training, comprehensive patching, and feature-level circuit discovery can require GPU-weeks. | Report: model size, GPU type and count, wall-clock time, number of forward passes, and total FLOPs for each major analysis step. Note which steps are parallelizable. Provide estimated cost for reproduction. | LLM describes "comprehensive activation patching over all heads and all positions" for a 7B parameter model as a straightforward step. This requires O(n_layers × n_heads × seq_len × dataset_size) forward passes — potentially millions of forward passes, requiring GPU-days even on A100s. |
| 45 | **Misinterpreting feature cosine similarity** | LLMs compare SAE features across different SAE training runs by cosine similarity of decoder vectors, but different runs may learn the same features in different orientations (due to symmetries in the loss function) or learn genuinely different decompositions of the same space. | When comparing features across SAE runs: (1) use a matching algorithm (e.g., linear assignment based on pairwise similarities) rather than greedy matching, (2) report the overall distribution of best-match similarities, not just a few high-similarity pairs, (3) verify that matched features activate on the same examples, not just have similar decoder directions. | LLM finds feature A (run 1) and feature B (run 2) with cosine similarity 0.95 and declares them "the same feature." But feature A activates on English text about cooking while feature B activates on restaurant reviews — the decoder directions happen to be similar because both involve food-related vocabulary, but they're functionally different features. |
| 46 | **Confusing loss landscape with feature geometry** | LLMs conflate the SAE's loss landscape (where the optimizer converges) with the geometry of the model's feature space. Multiple SAE training runs finding different features doesn't mean the model's features are "not real" — it may mean the SAE has multiple near-optimal solutions that tile the feature space differently. | Distinguish: (1) training instability (different runs give different features because the loss has many local optima) from (2) genuine feature non-uniqueness (the model's representation can be validly decomposed in multiple ways, like choosing different basis vectors for a subspace). Test by checking whether different decompositions explain the same model behaviors. | LLM trains two SAEs that find different features and concludes "the model doesn't have discrete features — they're artifacts of SAE training." But both sets of features can be used for circuit discovery and produce equally faithful circuits, suggesting the features are real but the decomposition is non-unique (like choosing different bases for the same subspace). |
| 47 | **Incorrect attention head composition analysis** | LLMs analyze how attention heads compose (head A writes information that head B reads) but compute the composition score incorrectly. Proper QK and OV composition requires multiplying the correct weight matrices in the correct order, accounting for the residual connection. | Composition score for head A (layer l) composing into head B (layer l'): QK composition = W_Q^B @ W_OV^A (how much A's output affects B's attention pattern). OV composition = W_OV^B @ W_OV^A (how much A's output, processed by B, affects the final output). Verify by comparing these scores against empirical path-patching results. | LLM computes the "composition score" as cosine similarity between head A's output and head B's query direction. This is a crude approximation that ignores the actual weight matrices. The correct computation involves the full OV and QK matrices and can give a different ranking of composing head pairs. |
| 48 | **Ignoring skip connections in circuit analysis** | LLMs trace circuits through attention heads and MLPs but forget that the residual stream provides a direct path that bypasses these components. Information deposited at layer L can persist in the residual stream and be read at layer L+5 without passing through any intermediate attention heads or MLPs. | Always include the residual stream "identity path" when tracing circuits. A complete circuit description includes both component-mediated paths (through specific heads/MLPs) and direct residual paths. Test: does ablating the hypothesized circuit fully remove the behavior, or does a residual path preserve partial performance? | LLM identifies a circuit: "head 3.0 → head 7.1 → head 9.6 → output." But actually, head 3.0 writes to the residual stream, and its signal is read directly at the output via the unembedding (no intermediate heads needed). The circuit through heads 7.1 and 9.6 is an additional processing path, not the only path. |
| 49 | **Using frozen activations from the wrong forward pass** | LLMs cache activations from one forward pass and reuse them in a patching experiment with a different input, forgetting that activations at later layers depend on earlier layers. Patching layer 5 with cached activations changes the computation at layers 6-12, making the cached activations at those layers stale. | When patching: (1) if patching one site, re-run the model forward from that site to get fresh downstream activations, or (2) if using cached activations, only cache from the forward pass that matches the current intervention. Never mix activations from different forward passes at adjacent layers without re-running the model. | LLM caches all activations from a clean run, then patches layer 5 from the corrupted run, but reads the "post-patching" layer 8 activations from the CLEAN cache instead of re-running layers 5-8. The layer 8 activations are wrong because they don't reflect the patched layer 5 values. |
| 50 | **Misinterpreting feature frequency as importance** | LLMs assume features that activate frequently are more important than rare features. But frequency and importance are orthogonal: a feature that activates on 50% of tokens (e.g., "is a common English word") may contribute minimally to specific predictions, while a feature that activates on 0.1% of tokens (e.g., "Python list comprehension syntax") may be critical for those specific predictions. | Measure importance by behavioral effect (patching, steering) rather than activation frequency. Report both frequency and importance. A complete characterization includes: (1) density (how often it activates), (2) mean activation (how strongly it fires), and (3) causal importance (how much behavior changes when it's modified). | LLM ranks features by activation frequency and presents the top-10 as "the most important features in the model." Feature #1 (activates on 60% of tokens) turns out to encode something like "current position is not the first token" — ubiquitous but uninteresting. Meanwhile, feature #8347 (activates on 0.05% of tokens) is the key feature for the model's code generation capability. |

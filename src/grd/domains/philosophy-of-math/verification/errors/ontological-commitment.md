# Ontological Commitment Errors

## Description
Errors in reasoning about the existence, nature, and status of mathematical
objects — the central questions of mathematical ontology.

## Symptoms
- Arguments that assume mathematical objects must be either fully abstract Platonic entities or
  mere fictions, ignoring intermediate positions (structuralism, thin realism).
- Ontological conclusions drawn from purely mathematical results without philosophical argument.
- Equivocation between different senses of "existence" (existential quantifier in a formal system
  vs. metaphysical existence claims).
- Treating Quine's criterion of ontological commitment (to be is to be the value of a bound
  variable) as the only way to read off ontological commitments from theories.

## Common Causes
- Insufficient familiarity with the range of ontological positions available.
- Conflating the existential quantifier's formal role with metaphysical existence claims.
- Treating the indispensability argument as decisive without examining its contested premises.
- Assuming that if a mathematical entity is useful, it must "really exist."
- Projecting a physicalist ontology onto mathematical discourse — expecting mathematical
  objects to have causal powers, spatiotemporal location, etc.

## Prevention
- Map the full space of ontological positions before constructing or evaluating arguments.
- Distinguish between quantifier commitment (what the theory says exists) and ontological
  commitment (what we believe exists based on the theory).
- Engage with at least three distinct ontological positions (e.g., platonism, nominalism,
  structuralism) rather than treating the debate as binary.
- Make the criterion of ontological commitment explicit — Quinean, deflationary, or other.
- Test whether the argument's conclusions change under different ontological frameworks.

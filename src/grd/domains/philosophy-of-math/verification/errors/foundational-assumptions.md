# Foundational Assumption Errors

## Description
Errors arising from uncritical acceptance of, or confusion about, foundational
frameworks — treating the choice of foundation as philosophically neutral or
conflating different senses of "foundation."

## Symptoms
- Treating ZFC as "the" foundation of mathematics rather than one possible framework.
- Assuming that foundational questions are settled and philosophically uninteresting.
- Arguing that mathematics "is" set theory because it can be formalized in ZFC.
- Conflating the technical sense of "foundation" (a system in which other mathematics
  can be developed) with the epistemic sense (a secure basis for mathematical knowledge).
- Rejecting alternative foundations (type theory, category theory) as "non-standard"
  without philosophical argument.

## Common Causes
- Mathematical training that presents ZFC as the default without discussing alternatives.
- Confusing "can be formalized in X" with "is fundamentally about X's objects."
- Not distinguishing between foundation-as-framework (organizational) and
  foundation-as-justification (epistemic/philosophical).
- Ignoring the historical contingency of ZFC's dominance — it emerged from specific
  responses to the set-theoretic paradoxes, not from philosophical inevitability.
- Assuming that the choice of foundation is merely a matter of convenience with no
  philosophical implications.

## Prevention
- Acknowledge that multiple foundational frameworks exist (ZFC, type theory, category
  theory, HoTT) and that choice among them is philosophically significant.
- Distinguish explicitly between mathematical foundations (technical frameworks) and
  philosophical foundations (epistemic/ontological justification).
- When assuming a particular foundation, state this as an explicit convention and assess
  whether the philosophical conclusions depend on this choice.
- Consider foundational pluralism — the view that different foundations may be appropriate
  for different purposes — as a live option.
- Test whether arguments survive translation between foundational frameworks.

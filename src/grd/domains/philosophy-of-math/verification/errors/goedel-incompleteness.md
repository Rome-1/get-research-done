# Goedel Incompleteness Misapplication Errors

## Description
Misuses and overextensions of Goedel's first and second incompleteness theorems
in philosophical argumentation — one of the most common sources of error in
philosophy of mathematics and beyond.

## Symptoms
- Claims that Goedel "showed mathematics is incomplete" without specifying which system.
- Arguments that human minds transcend formal systems because humans can "see" the Goedel
  sentence is true (Lucas-Penrose argument) without addressing the extensive rebuttals.
- Assertions that the incompleteness theorems refute Hilbert's program entirely, when they
  only refute the finitary consistency proof component.
- Uses of "Goedel's theorem" in contexts where the formal prerequisites (consistency,
  recursive axiomatizability, sufficient strength) are not satisfied.
- Claims that incompleteness means mathematical truth is "unknowable" or "subjective."

## Common Causes
- Knowing the slogans ("any sufficiently strong system is incomplete") without knowing the
  precise statement and its conditions.
- Confusing incompleteness (not all truths are provable in a given system) with uncertainty
  (we cannot be sure of anything) or undecidability (no algorithm decides membership).
- Conflating the first theorem (incompleteness) with the second (unprovability of consistency).
- Not distinguishing between the Goedel sentence being true (in the standard model) and
  being provable (in the system) — a use/mention confusion.
- Treating "formal system" as synonymous with "any system of rules or principles."
- Ignoring that the theorems apply to specific formal systems, not to human mathematical
  practice as a whole.

## Prevention
- Always state which incompleteness theorem is being invoked and check its precise hypotheses.
- Verify that the system in question is: (a) consistent, (b) recursively axiomatizable,
  (c) at least as strong as Robinson arithmetic (Q).
- Distinguish sharply between: truth in the standard model, provability in a formal system,
  and knowability by human mathematicians.
- Engage with the technical literature on the Lucas-Penrose argument (Putnam, Boolos, Shapiro,
  Koellner) before invoking or dismissing it.
- Note that incompleteness is relative to a system — adding the Goedel sentence as an axiom
  produces a new system with its own Goedel sentence.
- Remember: consistency is not undermined by incompleteness. An incomplete system can be
  perfectly consistent.

# Proof Theory Errors

## Description
Errors about the nature, status, and role of mathematical proof — confusing
different notions of proof, misunderstanding the relationship between formal
and informal proof, and mischaracterizing proof-theoretic results.

## Symptoms
- Treating informal mathematical proofs as mere abbreviations of formal derivations.
- Claiming that computer-verified proofs are epistemically superior to human proofs (or
  vice versa) without articulating the relevant epistemological criteria.
- Asserting that diagrammatic reasoning is inherently unreliable or non-rigorous.
- Confusing proof in a formal system with proof in mathematical practice.
- Misinterpreting the significance of long or complex proofs (classification of finite
  simple groups, Hales's proof of Kepler conjecture) for philosophy of proof.

## Common Causes
- Conflating the formal notion of proof (a finite sequence of formulas derived by rules)
  with the social notion (an argument accepted by the mathematical community).
- Assuming the formalizability thesis (every acceptable proof can be formalized) without
  recognizing it as a substantive philosophical claim.
- Not distinguishing between proof as product (the written argument) and proof as process
  (the activity of proving).
- Ignoring the epistemological role of understanding — a proof that convinces without
  conveying understanding raises different philosophical questions than one that explains.
- Treating all proofs as having the same epistemic character regardless of their method
  (constructive vs. classical, elementary vs. abstract, human vs. computational).

## Prevention
- Specify which notion of proof is being discussed: formal derivation, rigorous informal
  proof, proof sketch, diagrammatic proof, computational verification.
- When invoking the formalizability thesis, state it as an explicit premise and note
  the known challenges (e.g., proofs relying on visual reasoning, proofs using novel
  concepts not yet formalized).
- Distinguish between the certainty a proof provides and the understanding it conveys.
- Assess computational proofs (four color theorem, Kepler conjecture) on their own terms
  rather than by the standards of traditional proof.
- Recognize that the philosophical significance of proof varies across mathematical
  domains — proof in analysis differs from proof in algebra differs from proof in
  combinatorics.

# Historical Contextualization Protocol

## Purpose
Properly situate a philosophical argument about mathematics in its historical
and intellectual context, avoiding anachronism while drawing legitimate
connections to contemporary debates.

## Steps

1. **Fix the historical period** — Lock `historical_context`. Identify the
   relevant era: ancient (Plato, Aristotle, Euclid), early modern (Descartes,
   Leibniz, Kant), foundational crisis (Frege, Russell, Hilbert, Brouwer,
   Goedel), post-Goedel (Quine, Putnam, Benacerraf), contemporary (Shapiro,
   Hellman, Field, Maddy, Leng).
2. **Reconstruct the intellectual context** — What mathematical developments
   were available? What philosophical problems were salient? What conceptual
   resources existed? Key contextual factors:
   (a) Ancient: no symbolic algebra, no formal logic, geometry as paradigm.
   (b) Early modern: analytic geometry, calculus, no rigorous foundations.
   (c) Foundational crisis: non-Euclidean geometry, set-theoretic paradoxes,
       formalization of logic, Goedel's theorems.
   (d) Contemporary: independence results, category theory, computer-assisted
       proofs, structural approaches.
3. **Use primary sources** — Base interpretation on the philosopher's own
   texts, not secondary summaries. Note the original language and key
   terminological choices. Identify what the philosopher actually said versus
   what later interpreters attributed to them.
4. **Identify anachronism risks** — Check for:
   (a) Attributing modern technical concepts (computability, model, structure)
       to figures who predated them.
   (b) Evaluating historical arguments against modern standards they couldn't
       have met.
   (c) Reading modern positions back into historical texts (Plato as a
       "mathematical platonist," Hilbert as a "game formalist").
   (d) Ignoring aspects of historical views that don't fit modern categories
       (Brouwer's mysticism, Frege's concept-script metaphysics).
5. **Draw legitimate connections** — Distinguish between:
   (a) Direct influence (X read Y and responded).
   (b) Structural similarity (X and Y face analogous problems).
   (c) Retrospective reconstruction (we can see X as anticipating Y, but X
       didn't see it that way).
   Label each connection type explicitly.
6. **Assess historical significance** — What was the argument's impact on
   subsequent philosophy? Did it change the terms of debate? Was it
   understood by contemporaries? Was it rediscovered or reinterpreted later?
7. **Bridge to contemporary relevance** — If connecting historical work to
   current debates, make the bridging argument explicit. What survives
   transplantation to the modern context? What doesn't?

## Common Pitfalls
- Treating the "standard narrative" (logicism-formalism-intuitionism as
  exhaustive foundational schools) as historically accurate.
- Ignoring the mathematical context (what was provable at the time).
- Conflating what a philosopher said with what is now attributed to them.
- Assuming that because we have better tools, earlier views are simply wrong.
- Cherry-picking quotes to support a modern reading.

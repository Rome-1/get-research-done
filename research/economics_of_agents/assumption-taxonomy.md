**Status:** First draft survey
**Date:** 2026-03-29
**Author:** Alice (GetResearch)

## Introduction

Modern economics is built on theorems that assume their agents are human. This is not a stylistic choice or a simplifying convenience — it is structural. The major results in general equilibrium, mechanism design, game theory, monetary economics, and labor economics all depend on properties that biological agents happen to satisfy: each agent has a unique, costly-to-forge identity; computation is bounded and slow; preferences are consistent within a decision-maker; labor supply is ultimately constrained by the number of living people; and transaction speed is limited by physical and cognitive friction. These properties were so universal in the history of economic thought that they were rarely stated explicitly. They functioned as implicit axioms — background conditions so obviously true that formalizing them seemed pedantic.

AI agents violate all of these properties simultaneously. A single principal can instantiate thousands of agents in seconds. Each agent can process information and execute transactions at machine speed. Agent preferences are programmatic and can be rewritten between interactions or made deliberately inconsistent to exploit mechanism design. Labor supply becomes a function of compute cost rather than population. Transaction velocity is bounded only by network latency and API rate limits. The result is not a marginal perturbation to existing models — it is a wholesale violation of the substrate assumptions on which the major theorems rest. When identity is cheap, sybil-freeness fails. When computation is fast, equilibrium concepts built on bounded rationality dissolve. When labor supply is elastic to compute, classical wage theory breaks down.

This document provides a systematic taxonomy of these violations. For each major economic result, we identify the specific assumptions that AI agents break, describe the agent capability responsible, and assess the severity of the resulting breakdown. The goal is not to claim that economics is wrong — the theorems are mathematically valid — but to map precisely where the premises fail in agent-populated economies, and therefore where the conclusions no longer hold. This is the prerequisite for building new theory that actually describes markets with artificial participants.

## Deep Dives

### Sybil Attacks on Mechanism Design

The central vulnerability is this: nearly every mechanism in the MD literature implicitly assumes that each participant is a unique entity whose identity is exogenous to the mechanism. This assumption was so natural in the physical world that Hurwicz, Myerson, and Maskin never bothered to formalize it. Douceur (2002) was the first to name the problem in a distributed systems context — the "Sybil attack" — but the economic implications run far deeper than peer-to-peer networks.

Consider VCG specifically. The mechanism charges each agent the externality they impose on others: agent i pays the difference between the social welfare of all other agents with and without i's participation. This is elegant when agents are atomic. But suppose a single principal controls agents i_1, ..., i_k that collectively value a good at V. In the single-agent case, the principal pays their full externality. In the sybil case, each sub-agent i_j has a small individual value, imposes a small externality, and pays accordingly. The total payment across sybils can be made strictly less than the single-agent payment. The extraction condition is straightforward: if the cost of creating k identities is c(k) and the surplus extracted by splitting is s(k) = payment_single - payment_sybil(k), the attack is profitable when s(k) > c(k). For AI agents, c(k) is approximately k times the cost of one API call or one VM-hour. As inference costs drop (they have fallen roughly 10x per year since 2023), c(k) approaches zero, and virtually every VCG instance becomes sybil-vulnerable. This is not a theoretical curiosity — it means VCG cannot be deployed in any market where AI agents participate without a robust (and currently nonexistent) identity layer.

The problem generalizes beyond VCG. Any mechanism where an agent's payment or allocation depends on the number of competing agents is sybil-vulnerable. This includes all standard auctions (first-price, second-price, English, Dutch), scoring rules used for information elicitation, and matching mechanisms like deferred acceptance when priority scores depend on participant counts. The design space of sybil-proof mechanisms is an open and largely unexplored area. Existing results (e.g., Yokoo et al. 2004 on false-name-proof combinatorial auctions) suggest that sybil-proofness imposes severe efficiency costs — you may not be able to have both.

### The Identity Cost Function

We propose that identity cost should be treated as a first-class economic variable, analogous to transaction cost or information cost. Define c(k) as the total cost for a single principal to maintain k distinct identities that are recognized as separate agents by economic mechanisms. We can decompose this as:

c(k) = c_0 + c_marginal * (k - 1) + c_coordination(k)

where c_0 is the cost of the first (base) identity, c_marginal is the cost of each additional identity, and c_coordination(k) is the cost of coordinating behavior across k identities to avoid detection. In traditional economics, c_0 was the cost of being born, raised, and socialized — effectively infinite and certainly exogenous. c_marginal was the cost of maintaining a second legal identity — fraud penalties, cognitive overhead, physical presence requirements — also very high. c_coordination was bounded by human cognitive limits on managing multiple personas. The entire function was so steep that economists never wrote it down.

For AI agents: c_0 is one API key registration (minutes, dollars). c_marginal is one additional API call or container instantiation (milliseconds, fractions of a cent). c_coordination(k) may be literally zero if all k identities are controlled by a single program. The function has collapsed from effectively infinite to effectively linear with a near-zero slope. This is not a quantitative change — it is a qualitative phase transition in the structure of economic interaction. Every result that implicitly assumed c(k) = infinity for k > 1 needs to be re-examined with c(k) approximately equal to epsilon * k. The research program is: for each classical result, find the critical value of c_marginal below which the result breaks, and compare it to the actual c_marginal achievable with current AI technology. Our conjecture is that for most mechanism design results, the critical threshold was crossed somewhere around 2024-2025.

### Algorithmic Collusion Without Communication

Classical antitrust theory and game theory both treat collusion as requiring communication: firms must coordinate on prices, agree on market division, or exchange private information. The legal framework reflects this — price-fixing requires evidence of agreement. But AI agents can achieve collusive outcomes without any communication, through a mechanism that has no clean legal or economic precedent.

When multiple agents share the same architecture (e.g., they are all instances of the same foundation model, or trained on the same data), their strategy spaces are correlated not by communication but by construction. Calvano et al. (2020) demonstrated this empirically: Q-learning agents trained independently on the same pricing game consistently converged to supra-competitive prices. They developed reward-punishment strategies resembling tacit collusion — deviating agents were "punished" by competitors lowering prices — without any explicit coordination protocol. The agents had no shared state, no communication channel, and no instruction to collude. They converged to collusion because their learning dynamics, operating on the same environment structure, found the same basin of attraction. This result has been replicated across multiple settings and agent architectures (Assad et al. 2024, Johnson et al. 2023). The mechanism is robust: it does not require identical agents, merely similar enough optimization landscapes. As the economy fills with agents built on a small number of foundation model families, the preconditions for algorithmic collusion without communication are met by default. The policy question — how do you regulate collusion with no agreement, no communication, and no intent? — remains entirely open.

### Elastic Labor and the Wage Collapse Singularity

Classical labor economics models the labor market as the intersection of a downward-sloping demand curve (marginal revenue product of labor) and an upward-sloping supply curve (marginal disutility of labor, anchored to population). The equilibrium wage reflects the scarcity of human workers willing and able to perform the work at that price. The supply curve's upward slope is guaranteed by biology: there are only so many people, each has only so many hours, and the disutility of additional hours increases.

AI labor supply follows entirely different dynamics. The supply curve for AI-substitutable cognitive labor is approximately flat at the marginal cost of inference (compute cost per task divided by tasks per unit compute), then becomes vertical at the current infrastructure capacity constraint. Critically, both the flat portion and the vertical asymptote shift over time: inference costs have dropped roughly 10x annually since large language models became commercially available, and infrastructure capacity has expanded continuously. Model this as: w_AI(t) = c_compute(t) / productivity(t), where c_compute falls exponentially and productivity rises (due to model improvements). The equilibrium wage for AI-substitutable tasks therefore falls toward zero on a trajectory determined by the rates of compute cost reduction and model capability improvement. For human workers performing substitutable tasks, the wage is bounded above by w_AI(t) plus any premium for human-specific attributes (trust, legal liability, embodied presence). As w_AI(t) falls, this ceiling compresses. The dynamics are not those of normal technological unemployment, where displaced workers retrain for new roles — they are those of a market where the supply curve for an entire class of labor is collapsing toward the x-axis. The speed of adjustment, not just the direction, matters: if the transition happens over decades, labor markets can adapt; if it happens over years (which current cost curves suggest), the adjustment problem is qualitatively different from any historical precedent.

### Machine-Speed Velocity and Monetary Instability

The quantity theory of money, MV = PQ, treats velocity (V) as a relatively stable parameter determined by institutional and behavioral factors: payment frequency, cash-holding habits, settlement cycles. Central banks rely on V being slow-moving to make monetary policy tractable — if V is stable, then controlling M (money supply) gives predictable influence over P (price level) and Q (real output).

AI agents transacting autonomously can increase V by several orders of magnitude. Consider a digital token economy where agents buy and sell services from each other: a token can change hands thousands of times per second, limited only by network throughput and consensus mechanism speed. This is not hypothetical — on-chain DeFi markets already exhibit velocity spikes during periods of high automated trading activity. In such an environment, V becomes a function of the number of active agents and their transaction frequency, both of which can change discontinuously (e.g., when a new agent swarm is deployed or an existing one is shut down). The implications for monetary policy are severe: even with constant M, sudden changes in the AI agent population can produce rapid swings in MV, which must be absorbed by some combination of P and Q adjustment. In digital token economies without central bank intervention, this manifests as extreme price volatility. In economies where AI agents interact with fiat currency systems (through APIs to payment processors, for instance), the velocity shock propagates into the broader monetary system. The traditional central banking toolkit — interest rates, reserve requirements, open market operations — is calibrated for velocity changes on the order of single-digit percentages per year. It is not designed for velocity that can double or halve in hours.

## Open Questions

1. **Is sybil-proof mechanism design fundamentally at odds with allocative efficiency?** Early results (Yokoo et al. 2004) suggest a steep tradeoff. If this is a general impossibility result, it means that any market or voting mechanism operating in an AI-agent-populated environment must sacrifice either identity integrity or efficiency. Formalizing the Pareto frontier of this tradeoff is a major open problem.

2. **What is the minimum identity cost c_marginal that preserves the major mechanism design results?** Each theorem presumably has a critical threshold below which its guarantees break. Computing these thresholds — and comparing them to actual AI agent deployment costs — would tell us exactly which results are already broken in practice and which still hold for now.

3. **Can legal/regulatory frameworks address algorithmic collusion without communication?** Current antitrust law requires evidence of agreement. If collusive outcomes arise from shared model architecture with no communication, no agreement, and no intent, the entire enforcement paradigm is inapplicable. Is there a principled legal theory that can distinguish "trained on the same data and therefore plays the same strategy" from "independently arrived at the same price"? Should there be?

4. **What monetary policy framework is appropriate when velocity is a technology variable?** If V in MV = PQ can change by orders of magnitude based on AI agent deployment decisions, conventional monetary targeting is unworkable. Do we need new monetary instruments that directly regulate transaction velocity? Is a "velocity tax" (a per-transaction fee calibrated to dampen V) feasible without destroying the efficiency gains of machine-speed markets?

5. **Does the elastic AI labor supply produce a new stable equilibrium, or a transition to a qualitatively different economic regime?** Standard models predict that as wages for substitutable tasks fall, displaced workers move to complementary tasks where human comparative advantage persists. But if AI capabilities expand faster than humans can identify and retrain for new comparative advantages, the "always new tasks" argument may not hold. Is there a formal model that characterizes the conditions under which perpetual reallocation fails?

6. **How do we define and measure market concentration when agent identity is fluid?** Antitrust analysis relies on counting firms, measuring market share, and computing HHI. If a single principal can operate through thousands of apparently independent agents, traditional concentration metrics are meaningless. What data and methods would allow regulators to measure "effective concentration" — the degree of coordinated control — in agent-populated markets?

7. **Can general equilibrium theory be extended to endogenous agent sets?** Arrow-Debreu assumes a fixed, finite set of agents. If agents can be created and destroyed as a strategic choice, we need existence (and welfare) theorems for games where the player set is itself an equilibrium outcome. Some work exists in the entry/exit literature (Mankiw and Whinston 1986) but nothing at the scale of full general equilibrium with unbounded agent creation. Is existence even guaranteed in this setting?
